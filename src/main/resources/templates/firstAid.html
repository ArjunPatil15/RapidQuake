First Aid Tips for Earthquakes

Essential medical guidance for treating common earthquake-related injuries

Important Disclaimer

This information is for educational purposes only and does not replace professional medical advice. In case of serious injuries, always call 911 or seek immediate medical attention. If someone is unconscious, not breathing, or has severe bleeding, call emergency services immediately.

Assess the Situation: ABC Priority

A - Airway

Ensure the victim's airway is clear. Check for any obstructions in the mouth or throat. Tilt the head back gently to open the airway if the person is unconscious.

B - Breathing

Check if the person is breathing. Look for chest movement, listen for breath sounds, and feel for air from the nose or mouth. Begin CPR if not breathing.

C - Circulation

Check for a pulse and look for signs of severe bleeding. Control any major bleeding immediately with direct pressure on the wound.

Severe Bleeding

  1. 1
    Apply Direct Pressure Use a clean cloth or sterile gauze and press firmly on the wound. Maintain pressure for at least 10-15 minutes without lifting to check.
  2. 2
    Elevate if Possible Raise the injured area above the heart level if there's no suspected fracture. This helps reduce blood flow to the area.
  3. 3
    Bandage Securely Once bleeding slows, apply a bandage firmly but not so tight that it cuts off circulation. Check fingers/toes for color and warmth.
  4. 4
    Seek Medical Help Call 911 if bleeding doesn't stop after 15 minutes of pressure, if the wound is deep, or if caused by a puncture injury.

Fractures & Breaks

  1. 1
    Don't Move the Person Keep the injured person still. Movement can worsen a fracture or cause a simple break to become compound.
  2. 2
    Immobilize the Area Use a splint or rolled newspaper/magazine to immobilize the injured area. Pad the splint with cloth for comfort.
  3. 3
    Apply Ice Wrap ice in cloth and apply to reduce swelling. Never apply ice directly to skin. Use 20 minutes on, 20 minutes off.
  4. 4
    Watch for Shock Keep the person warm, calm, and lying down. Monitor breathing and consciousness while waiting for help.

Head Injuries

  1. 1
    Keep Person Still Don't move someone with a suspected head injury unless in immediate danger. Movement can worsen neck or spine injuries.
  2. 2
    Control Bleeding Apply gentle pressure with clean cloth to any bleeding scalp wounds. Don't apply pressure if you suspect a skull fracture.
  3. 3
    Monitor Consciousness Watch for confusion, drowsiness, vomiting, or unequal pupil size. These are signs of serious injury requiring immediate medical attention.
  4. 4
    Call 911 Immediately Any head injury with loss of consciousness, confusion, or severe bleeding requires emergency medical care.

Burns

  1. 1
    Cool the Burn Run cool (not cold) water over the burn for 10-20 minutes. Never use ice, as it can cause further damage to tissue.
  2. 2
    Remove Jewelry Take off rings, watches, or tight clothing near the burn before swelling occurs. Don't remove clothing stuck to the burn.
  3. 3
    Cover with Clean Cloth Use a sterile, non-stick bandage or clean cloth. Don't apply ointments, butter, or home remedies to serious burns.
  4. 4
    Seek Medical Care Get medical help for burns larger than 3 inches, on face/hands/feet/genitals, or if the burn appears deep with white or charred skin.

Puncture Wounds

  1. 1
    Don't Remove Object If an object is embedded in the wound, leave it in place. Removing it could cause severe bleeding. Stabilize it with bandages.
  2. 2
    Clean Minor Wounds For small punctures, wash with soap and water. Let it bleed a little to help clean out bacteria from inside the wound.
  3. 3
    Apply Antibiotic Use antibiotic ointment on minor punctures and cover with a bandage. Change the bandage daily and watch for infection signs.
  4. 4
    Tetanus Shot Check Puncture wounds have high infection risk. See a doctor if your tetanus shot isn't current (within 5-10 years).

Shock

  1. 1
    Recognize the Signs Look for pale/clammy skin, rapid pulse, shallow breathing, confusion, or unconsciousness. Shock is life-threatening and requires immediate action.
  2. 2
    Lay Person Down Have the person lie flat on their back. Elevate their legs about 12 inches unless you suspect head, neck, or back injury.
  3. 3
    Keep Warm Cover the person with a blanket or coat. Don't overheat. Maintain normal body temperature and keep them calm and still.
  4. 4
    Call 911 Shock requires emergency medical treatment. Monitor breathing and pulse while waiting for help. Begin CPR if needed.

Important: What NOT to Do

Don't Move Spinal Injuries Never move someone with a suspected neck or back injury unless they're in immediate danger. Movement can cause permanent paralysis.

Don't Give Food or Drink Don't give anything by mouth to someone who is unconscious, having trouble breathing, or may need surgery. This includes water.

Don't Remove Embedded Objects Objects stuck in wounds should only be removed by medical professionals. Removal can cause severe bleeding.

Don't Apply Tourniquets Unnecessarily Tourniquets should only be used as a last resort for life-threatening bleeding that can't be controlled by direct pressure.

Don't Use Home Remedies on Serious Burns Never put butter, oil, ice, or ointments on serious burns. These can cause infection and worsen tissue damage.

Don't Delay Emergency Care When in doubt, call 911. It's better to be safe than sorry. Professional medical help can make the difference between life and death.
