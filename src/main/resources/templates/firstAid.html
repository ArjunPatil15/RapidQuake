American Red Cross Guidelines

Earthquake First Aid Guide

Evidence-based emergency medical procedures for earthquake-related injuries

For Life-Threatening Emergencies: Call 911 Immediately

Medical Disclaimer

This information is for educational purposes only and does not constitute medical advice. Always call 911 for serious injuries including unconsciousness, difficulty breathing, severe bleeding, suspected spinal injury, or chest pain. When in doubt, seek professional medical help immediately.

ABC Assessment Priority

Always assess victims in this order before providing treatment

A

Airway

Check & Clear

  • Look for visible obstructions in mouth/throat
  • Tilt head back gently to open airway (if no neck injury)
  • Remove any visible foreign objects carefully
  • Never reach blindly into throat
If neck or spinal injury suspected, do NOT move head
B

Breathing

Assess Respiration

  • Look for chest rising and falling
  • Listen for breath sounds near nose/mouth
  • Feel for air movement on your cheek
  • Check for 10 seconds maximum
If not breathing: Begin CPR immediately (30 compressions, 2 breaths)
C

Circulation

Control Bleeding

  • Check for pulse at neck (carotid) or wrist (radial)
  • Look for severe bleeding
  • Assess skin color and temperature
  • Watch for signs of shock
Severe bleeding: Apply direct pressure immediately with clean cloth

Common Earthquake Injuries

Step-by-step treatment protocols for the most frequent injuries

CRITICAL

Severe Bleeding (Hemorrhage)

⏱️ Act within 3-5 minutes

Recognition

  • Blood spurting or flowing continuously
  • Blood soaking through clothing/bandages
  • Victim becoming pale, weak, or confused
  • Rapid, weak pulse

Treatment Protocol

1
Call 911 First

Have someone call emergency services immediately. If alone, call after applying pressure if victim is conscious.

2
Apply Direct Pressure

Use sterile gauze or clean cloth. Press firmly directly on wound for minimum 10-15 minutes without lifting to check. Use your full palm, not fingertips.

3
Elevate if Possible

Raise injured area above heart level ONLY if no suspected fracture. Keep applying pressure while elevating.

4
Apply Pressure Bandage

Once bleeding slows, apply pressure bandage firmly but not so tight it cuts off circulation. Check fingers/toes for warmth and color every 10 minutes.

5
Monitor for Shock

Keep victim lying down, warm but not hot. Do not give food or drink. Monitor breathing and consciousness until help arrives.

Do NOT

  • Remove objects embedded in wound - stabilize with bandages around it
  • Apply tourniquet unless life-threatening bleeding cannot be controlled
  • Peek at wound frequently - maintain pressure for full time
  • Use direct pressure on eye injuries, skull fractures, or protruding bones
SERIOUS

Fractures & Broken Bones

⏱️ Immobilize immediately

Recognition

  • Deformity, swelling, or abnormal positioning
  • Severe pain, especially with movement
  • Inability to bear weight or use limb
  • Grating sensation or sound (crepitus)
  • Bone protruding through skin (open fracture)

Treatment Protocol

1
Do Not Move Victim

Keep person completely still. Movement can turn simple fracture into compound fracture, causing severe complications.

2
Call 911

All suspected fractures require professional medical evaluation and X-rays for proper diagnosis.

3
Immobilize the Area

Splint above and below the injury. Use rigid materials (boards, magazines, cardboard). Pad splint with cloth for comfort. Secure with bandages or tape - firm but not tight.

4
Apply Ice

Wrap ice pack in cloth (never direct skin contact). Apply for 20 minutes, off 20 minutes. Reduces swelling and pain.

5
Monitor Circulation

Check area beyond injury every 15 minutes. Look for blue/white color, numbness, coldness, or tingling. Loosen splint if these occur.

Do NOT

  • Try to realign or straighten the bone
  • Push protruding bone back into wound
  • Move victim unless necessary for safety
  • Give food, drink, or medication by mouth
CRITICAL

Head & Brain Injuries

⏱️ Call 911 for any head injury with LOC

Danger Signs

  • Loss of consciousness (even briefly)
  • Confusion, disorientation, or amnesia
  • Severe headache that worsens
  • Unequal pupil sizes
  • Clear fluid from nose or ears
  • Repeated vomiting
  • Seizures
  • Slurred speech or vision problems

Treatment Protocol

1
Call 911 Immediately

Any head injury with loss of consciousness, confusion, or danger signs requires emergency medical care. Brain injury can worsen rapidly.

2
Keep Victim Still

Assume neck/spine injury until proven otherwise. Do not move head or neck. Stabilize head with hands on both sides if victim must be moved for safety.

3
Control Scalp Bleeding

Apply gentle pressure with clean cloth. Do NOT apply pressure if you suspect skull fracture (depression, soft area). Simply cover wound.

4
Monitor Continuously

Check consciousness level every 5 minutes. Note pupil size, breathing rate, and responsiveness. Report all changes to paramedics.

5
Prevent Shock

Keep victim lying flat (unless vomiting). Cover with blanket to maintain body temperature. Do not give anything by mouth.

Do NOT

  • Move victim unnecessarily
  • Remove any objects embedded in skull
  • Give aspirin or ibuprofen (increases bleeding risk)
  • Let victim fall asleep without medical clearance
  • Apply direct pressure to depressed skull fracture
SERIOUS

Burns (Thermal, Chemical, Electrical)

⏱️ Cool immediately for 10-20 minutes

Burn Classification

1st Degree: Red, painful, no blisters (like sunburn)
2nd Degree: Blisters, severe pain, red/white appearance
3rd Degree: White/charred skin, little/no pain (nerve damage) - CALL 911

Treatment Protocol

1
Stop the Burning

Remove from heat source. For fire: Stop, Drop, Roll. For chemical: Flush with water for 20 minutes. For electrical: Do not touch victim until power is off.

2
Cool the Burn

Run cool (not cold) water over burn for 10-20 minutes. Never use ice - causes tissue damage. Remove jewelry/tight clothing before swelling starts.

3
Assess & Call for Help

Call 911 if: burn larger than 3 inches, on face/hands/feet/genitals, appears white/charred, victim is child/elderly, or caused by chemicals/electricity.

4
Cover Burn

Use sterile, non-stick bandage or clean cloth. Do not use adhesive bandages. Do not apply ointments, butter, or home remedies to serious burns.

5
Prevent Shock

For large burns, treat for shock: lay victim flat, elevate feet 12 inches, cover with blanket. Monitor breathing and consciousness.

Do NOT

  • Apply ice, butter, oil, or ointments to serious burns
  • Break blisters - increases infection risk
  • Remove clothing stuck to burn
  • Touch burn with dirty hands or materials
  • Use fluffy cotton or material that sheds fibers
CRITICAL

Shock (Hypovolemic/Traumatic)

⏱️ Life-threatening - Act immediately

Recognition

  • Pale, cold, clammy skin
  • Rapid, weak pulse (over 100 bpm)
  • Rapid, shallow breathing
  • Weakness, dizziness, confusion
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Blue lips and fingernails
  • Altered consciousness or unresponsiveness

Treatment Protocol

1
Call 911 Immediately

Shock is life-threatening and requires immediate advanced medical care. Do not delay.

2
Position Victim

Lay person flat on back. Elevate legs 12 inches (unless head, neck, back, or leg injury suspected). This increases blood flow to vital organs.

3
Maintain Body Temperature

Cover with blanket to prevent heat loss. Do not overheat. Keep victim warm but not hot. Remove wet clothing if possible.

4
Control Bleeding

If shock is due to bleeding, apply direct pressure to wounds while maintaining shock position. Do not elevate bleeding limb above heart.

5
Monitor Continuously

Check breathing, pulse, and consciousness every 2-3 minutes. Be prepared to begin CPR if victim stops breathing. Reassure and keep calm.

Do NOT

  • Give anything by mouth (food, water, medication)
  • Move victim unnecessarily
  • Leave victim alone
  • Wait to see if condition improves - call 911 immediately
SERIOUS

Crush Injuries & Entrapment

⏱️ Do not remove heavy object without medical help

Recognition

  • Body part trapped under heavy object > 15 minutes
  • Severe pain in compressed area
  • Numbness or tingling
  • Pale or mottled skin beyond injury
  • Absence of pulse beyond crush site
Crush Syndrome Risk: Releasing pressure after prolonged crushing (>15 min) can release toxins causing cardiac arrest. Always call 911 BEFORE removing heavy objects from victims.

Treatment Protocol

1
Call 911 First

Do not remove crushing object until paramedics arrive if victim has been trapped >15 minutes. Sudden release can cause fatal complications.

2
Stabilize Object

If possible, stabilize crushing object to prevent further movement or pressure. Do not try to lift heavy objects alone - wait for rescue team.

3
Reassure Victim

Keep victim calm and still. Explain that help is coming. Monitor consciousness and breathing. Keep talking to maintain responsiveness.

4
After Release (Medical Personnel Present)

Once medical team releases pressure, immediately apply pressure bandage to any bleeding. Immobilize injured area. Monitor for shock symptoms.

Do NOT

  • Remove heavy objects if victim trapped >15 minutes without medical supervision
  • Give fluids before medical clearance
  • Attempt to free victim alone - get help

CPR Quick Reference

Hands-Only CPR (Adults)

Use if victim is unresponsive and not breathing normally

1

Call 911

Activate emergency services immediately.

2

Push Hard & Fast

100–120 compressions per minute in the center of the chest.

3

Allow Full Recoil

Let the chest fully rise between compressions.

4

Continue Until Help Arrives

Do not stop unless help takes over.
